because geht's is an optional contraction. Whereas the prepositional contractions are required with the exception of vom and beim. if you are saying 'in the' or 'to the' and the noun is masculine or neuter, you have to use im, zum, or zur. or when you say 'into the' and the noun is neuter, ins has to be used. zu dem, zu der, in dem, and in das just aren't said or used in writing except when explaining what the contracted forms stand for. ___ geht es is still grammatically acceptable and used, especially in formal speech. It's not just the one, by the way. A lot of times when you do a nonstandard sentence in which es is the subject, it gets contracted onto the verb with an 's.

As AbunPang put it, you certainly can say "ich bin gut", but it is not the same. I've heard from native German teachers (my professor was from a small town outside Munich) that "Ich bin gut" comes across as boasting about ones prowess in the bedroom. So stick with "Mir geht's gut", "Es geht mir gut", or "Gut geht's mir." depending on which thing you want to emphasize. That thing goes in the first position. The literal translation of this is "To/For Me, [it goes] well", "It goes to/for me well", or "Well [it goes] to/for me." The thing to remember for this is that translation is not always an exact word-for-word substitution. Sometimes the translator will translate the phrase or sentence's general meaning.

yes and no. but remember, here: geht's is a contraction of geht es. So you can say one of three things here: Ja, Mir geht's gut, Ja, Gut geht's mir, or Ja, Es geht mir gut. German is primarily a verb-II language. Which means that the verb itself must stay in the second position and the subject must be next to the verb. The only exception to this rule is when dealing with subordinating conjunctions. These are conjunctions which when used in a compound sentence force the verb to the end of the clause. I.E: "Weil ich auf deutsch sprach, verstanden die Amerikaner mich nicht." Or "Die Amerikaner verstanden mich nicht, weil ich auf deutsch sprach." or Mich verstanden die Amerikaner nicht, weil ich auf deutsch sprach.* Here we see that relative clauses will take position one if their said first. Then your verb comes, then the subject. Then the rest of the main clause's predicate.

That’s wrong I’m afraid. In a normal declarative sentence the conjugated verb (in this case geht) always has to come in second position. So in this case you could say:

Mir geht es (> geht's) gut.
Es geht mir gut.

"Mir" answers the question "To whom?" or "For whom?" It is equivalent to the "me" in "My mom gives me a flower." Here, "Mir geht's gut" is "for me, it's going well". Where we're accentuating "for me". "Geht's" is a contraction of "geht es" or "it goes". That's the rule. It's sometimes why I prefer to teach people the literal translation of a phrase, versus the intended meaning when someone is learning grammar. German is typically a SVO (subject verb object) language, but its declined (has cases) nature means that you can screw around with the components of the sentence as long as the verb stays in second position and the subject is next to the verb. "Es geht mir gut" = "Mir geht's gut" = "Gut geht's mir" The difference is that we're accentuating different parts of the sentence. Whatever goes in position one is accentuated.

So what exactly does Mir and geht's mean in English?

On their own, mir means "(to) me" and geht's = geht es means "it goes".

But it's probably best to learn mir geht's gut = "I'm fine; I'm doing well" as a fixed expression.

The words can, of course, be used in other sentences.

mir is used in other idioms (e.g. mir ist warm = I am warm; mir doch egal = I don't care) or in more straight-forward sentences (e.g. er gibt mir das Buch = he is giving me the book / he is giving the book to me).

geht is used literally (er geht jetzt nach Hause = he is going home now) and also figuratively (wie geht's? = how are you?; Hallo? Geht's noch? = Excuse me, are you out of your mind?).
